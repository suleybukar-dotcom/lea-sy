Wellness & Lifestyle

Slowness is a
practice, not a personality.

Notes on how I take care of myself — the routines that help, the habits I've built slowly, and the ongoing project of staying grounded in a fast-moving world.

Matin, appartement — Le Plateau
Movement

How I move my body.

01

Pilates

I started pilates in the spring of 2024, mostly on recommendation from a friend who said it had changed how she moved through the world. She was right. I go to a small studio in the Plateau three times a week — Tuesday and Thursday mornings before work, and Saturday afternoons when I can hold the time.

Pilates is the first form of exercise I've ever actually liked. I think it's because it demands presence in a way running never did for me. You can't zone out. You have to be in your body, aware of what each part of it is doing, which turns out to be a kind of meditation I can actually sustain.

I'm not advanced. I still have to think hard about my form. But I feel stronger than I've ever been in my adult life, and I walk differently — more grounded, less braced for impact.

02

Swimming

I swim at the Centre sportif de Rosemont on Saturday mornings, usually the 8am lane-swim session when there are maybe twelve people in the whole pool and the light through the skylights is still soft. I've been doing it since 2023, inconsistently at first, then with more regularity as I understood what it gave me.

Swimming is almost the opposite of pilates for me — it's where I go to not think. Forty-five minutes of laps, no phone, no music, just water and breath and the mechanical rhythm of turning at the wall. By the time I dry off and get dressed, something has reset. I don't fully understand the neuroscience of it and I don't need to.

It's also, practically, one of the most affordable things I do for my health. A municipal pool pass in Montréal is inexpensive. I think about that sometimes when wellness content online makes it feel like taking care of yourself requires a certain income.

03

Walking without a destination

This is the one that doesn't feel like exercise but probably does the most for me. On weekend afternoons — especially when I'm feeling contracted or overstimulated — I put on a coat, leave my phone on the table, and walk for an hour or so with no particular goal. Sometimes I bring my camera. Usually I don't.

Montréal is an incredibly walkable city if you live in the right neighbourhoods, which I do. Le Plateau to Mile End, Mile End through Outremont, down to the Plateau again. The route changes but the feeling is the same: by the time I get back I'm different. Not transformed — just loosened. Slightly more like myself.

I learned this when I was going through the worst of my burnout. I couldn't concentrate, couldn't read, couldn't make decisions. But I could walk. So I walked. It became a kind of therapy by accident.

Marche, automne
Structure

My routines — the real version.

These are not aspirational. They're what I actually do most days — with variation, imperfection, and the occasional day where none of it happens and that's fine too.

Morning

6:30
Wake up — no phone for 20 minutes

The hardest habit I've built. I leave my phone charging across the room so the first thing I reach for is not a screen. Most days this works. Some days it doesn't.

6:45
Journaling

Three pages, longhand, stream of consciousness. No topic, no structure. Just whatever is sitting in my head that morning. I've been doing this for two years and I can't imagine stopping.

7:15
Coffee — made at home

A V60 pour-over if I have time, a moka pot if I don't. I'm not precious about it but I do care about it. Coffee is one of my genuine pleasures and I protect it accordingly.

7:30
Reading (not news)

Twenty minutes of fiction or essays before the day starts. I tried reading news first thing for years and it made me anxious before I'd even brushed my teeth. Fiction is better.

8:00
Get ready, walk to work or open laptop

I work in-studio three days a week and from home the other two. Walk days are better days, generally.

Evening

18:00
Finish work — hard stop

I set a 6pm alarm on work days. When it goes off, I close my laptop. This took a long time to enforce. I still sometimes ignore it. But the intention matters.

18:30
Cook dinner or pick something up

I cook about four nights a week — simple things, nothing that takes more than 40 minutes. The act of cooking is itself decompressive. I put on a playlist and let my hands do something uncomplicated.

20:00
Unstructured time

This is non-negotiable. Film, reading, phone calls, editing photos, nothing. The content of this time is less important than the fact that it belongs entirely to me.

21:30
Wind-down — phone in the other room

Skincare, a bit of reading, sometimes a bath. I dim the lights at home after 9pm — it sounds fussy but it genuinely changes how I feel by the time I'm trying to sleep.

22:30
Sleep

Eight hours is not a luxury. After burnout I understood that this is the single most important thing I can do for every other aspect of my life. It goes first.

Mental Wellbeing

The less visible work.

I see a therapist every other week. I've been doing this for two years and it's one of the best decisions I've made. I don't say that as a promotional statement — I just think it's worth naming, because I spent a long time believing I didn't have a good enough reason to need it.

The burnout period taught me that I had outsourced my sense of self-worth to external markers — productivity, output, how busy I looked. Therapy gave me somewhere to start pulling that apart. It's slow work. It's still in progress. But I feel qualitatively different than I did two years ago.

Journaling is the other thing. Writing every morning has become the closest thing I have to a daily therapy session between sessions. It doesn't replace anything, but it does let me hear myself clearly before the day adds noise.

Reading

What's on my shelf.

I read fiction before bed, non-fiction and essays in the mornings. These are some books that have genuinely changed how I think.

Currently reading
Outline
Rachel Cusk

A novel structured almost entirely as conversations. Quiet and devastating in equal measure. I've been reading it slowly on purpose.

Wellness
Why We Sleep
Matthew Walker

The book that made me stop treating sleep as a variable. Dense with science but genuinely readable. Changed my behaviour immediately.

Essays
Trick Mirror
Jia Tolentino

The essay on "always be optimising" basically described my twenties to me. I've re-read it twice. Uncomfortably accurate.

Fiction
Giovanni's Room
James Baldwin

Short and completely devastating. Baldwin's sentences are unlike anything else. I finished it in two sittings and sat with it for a week.

Growth
The Artist's Way
Julia Cameron

I resisted this for years because the title felt corny. I was wrong. The morning pages practice alone was worth it — it's what eventually led to my journaling habit.

Philosophy
How to Live
Sarah Bakewell

A biography of Montaigne structured around the question "how should one live?" Warm, funny, genuinely wise. Perfect for slow reading.
