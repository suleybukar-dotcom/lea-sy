Learning to slow down in a city that never asks you to

There's a specific kind of guilt that comes with choosing to do nothing in a place where everyone seems to be doing something. I noticed it first on a Sunday afternoon in February — I was sitting by my window watching it snow and I felt this low-level anxiety, like I was wasting something. I wasn't. I was just sitting. But somewhere along the way I had absorbed the idea that stillness needed to be earned, that rest was a reward rather than a baseline. I've been slowly working on that.

What burnout actually taught me about myself

It wasn't dramatic. There was no single breaking point, no moment where I collapsed or cried at my desk. It was just a slow erosion — until one Thursday morning I stared at my screen and felt genuinely nothing. Not tired. Not frustrated. Nothing. I had been working at a pace I could not sustain for longer than I realised, and my body and brain had quietly made a decision without me. What came after was uncomfortable: the acknowledgement that I had been outsourcing my sense of worth to how productive I was. That was the real lesson.

Why I started taking photos again

I'd stopped for almost two years without really noticing. Phone photos, yes — but not real looking, not the kind where you slow down and actually choose a frame. Then one November afternoon on rue Rachel, the light hit the wet pavement after rain and I reached for my phone and it felt wrong. Too easy. Too flat. I went home, ordered a Sony α7C secondhand, and waited three weeks for it to arrive. When it did, something that had been quietly dormant in me woke up. Photography is how I pay attention. I'd forgotten that.

Soft life isn't a trend. It's what I built for myself.

I get a little tired of how the internet talks about "soft life." The phrase has been aestheticised to the point where it's almost meaningless — filtered into pastel reels of girls with no jobs and unlimited brunch budgets. That's not what it means to me. Soft life, in my understanding, is the absence of unnecessary hardship. It's the discipline of not making things harder than they need to be. After years of surviving on anxiety and adrenaline, learning to let things be easy was actually the harder thing.

My evening routine — the real, unglamorous version

I want to be honest with you: my evenings are not particularly Instagram-worthy. There is no carefully arranged herbal tea tray. Sometimes I eat dinner standing at the kitchen counter because I forgot to set the table again. But there is a shape to my evenings that I've built slowly over the past year, and it's made a real difference. Not because it's a perfect routine, but because it exists at all — and because having a loose structure for the end of the day means my brain knows when it's allowed to stop working.

Living alone changed everything I thought I knew about myself

I moved into my own apartment eighteen months ago. Before that I'd always had roommates — out of financial necessity, mostly, but also because the idea of living alone felt like something I wasn't ready for yet. What I didn't expect was how quickly it became the thing I was most grateful for. Living alone taught me what I actually like, without anyone else's habits or preferences in the mix. My music. My hours. My silence. It also made me realise how much of who I was had been shaped in response to other people. Alone, I had to figure out who I was on my own terms.

Things I wish someone had told me at 21

At 21 I was working a closing shift at a grocery store most Fridays, waking up at 7am for 8h30 classes, and trying to convince myself I was fine. I wasn't not fine exactly — but I was running on a kind of determined ignorance that I now recognise as coping. Here are the things I would tell that person, not to fix anything, but just so she'd know: the financial stress does not last forever. The tiredness is real, not weakness. The friendships that disappear under pressure were not your fault. And you don't have to have a plan as long as you have a direction.

Finding balance in a world that rewards being busy

In marketing especially, there's a culture of constant availability that gets mistaken for dedication. I bought into it for a long time. Responding to Slack at 10pm. Reworking decks on weekends. Treating every deadline like an emergency whether it was one or not. What I've slowly come to understand is that this pace is not sustainable, and more importantly — it's not actually better work. My best ideas come when I'm not trying to have them. The campaigns I'm most proud of were built by a version of me who got eight hours of sleep.
